Background

Introduction

Aviation is globalization made visible — millions of people moving across borders every day.

Globalization refers to the increasing interconnectedness of countries through the movement of people, goods, services, and information. One of the most visible infrastructures supporting globalization is the global aviation network, which connects cities and regions through international flight routes.

Before the COVID-19 pandemic, air travel played a crucial role in enabling global mobility, supporting tourism, international education, migration, and business activities. Major airport hubs functioned as key nodes within a highly integrated global system.

Why This Topic?

The global aviation network was selected as the focus of this project because it provides a clear and measurable representation of globalization in practice. Flight routes connect countries physically, while passenger flows reflect patterns of economic activity, tourism, and human mobility.

The COVID-19 pandemic represents one of the most significant disruptions to globalization in recent history. Travel restrictions and border closures led to a dramatic reduction in global mobility, making aviation an ideal case study to analyze how global systems respond to crisis.

Timeline

Global Flight Network Transformation

A chronological analysis of how global flight networks evolved before, during, and after the pandemic — revealing the vulnerability and resilience of globalization.

2018 – 2019 · Before COVID-19

The Connected Global Network

Prior to the pandemic, the global flight network was highly developed and densely interconnected, reflecting strong globalization and increasing international mobility. Major hub airports such as Singapore Changi, Dubai International, and London Heathrow played a central role in linking Europe to global destinations, particularly for business and long-haul travel. Bali's Ngurah Rai Airport functioned as a tourism gateway, relying heavily on international visitors.

Dense global connections High passenger demand Hubs vs tourism airports

2020 – 2021 · During COVID-19

Disruption of Global Mobility

The outbreak of COVID-19 caused a major disruption to global flight networks due to border closures, travel restrictions, and quarantine policies. This impact varied across airports. Singapore Changi and London Heathrow experienced sharp declines due to strict travel controls, while Dubai International maintained partial connectivity and recovered earlier. Bali Airport was among the most severely affected, as international tourism nearly stopped completely.

Collapse of international air travel Significant decline in passengers Strong government control

2022 – 2024 · After COVID-19

Recovery and Transformation

From 2022 onwards, the global aviation sector began to recover as travel restrictions were gradually lifted. Passenger demand increased significantly, driven by the reopening of international borders and renewed demand for travel. However, recovery was uneven across regions. Major global hubs such as Dubai recovered more rapidly, while tourism-dependent destinations experienced slower growth. The pandemic also introduced long-term changes including digital health verification systems, increased awareness of health risks, and shifts in travel behavior.

Gradual reopening Uneven recovery Digital health passports

2017–2019 · Pre-Pandemic Baseline

Steady, predictable global growth

Atlanta (ATL) consistently leads the world above 100M passengers. Dubai (DXB) and London (LHR) track closely together in the high 80s, while Bali (DPS) grows steadily from 21M.

2020 · The Global Collapse

Total synchronization — every line plummets

London (LHR) and Bali (DPS) drop by over 73%, marking the physical "collapse" of globalization. Every single airport line falls simultaneously — a uniquely global event.

2021 · The Year of Divergence

Atlanta & Dubai pivot upward; Bali bottoms

The most revealing year: domestic ATL and strategic DXB immediately recover. London declines to its lowest (19.4M). Bali bottoms at a mere 3.7M due to strict island border closures.

2022–2025 · The New Global Normal

Dubai outpaces pre-pandemic self

By 2023, Dubai and Atlanta successfully claw back to — and exceed — their 2019 baselines. London settles into a plateau slightly below its pre-pandemic peak. Bali recovers but more slowly.

About

Project Overview

This project examines how the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted and transformed global aviation networks — one of the most measurable expressions of globalization. By tracking passenger volumes at four strategically chosen airports, we can observe how an external shock cascades through an integrated global system and then diverges unevenly in recovery.

The choice of ATL, DXB, LHR, and DPS is deliberate: they represent distinct node types. Atlanta is a domestic-led mega-hub, Dubai is a global transfer hub, Heathrow is a policy-sensitive international gateway, and Bali is a pure tourism sink. Together they tell the full story.

Through this lens, aviation becomes more than transportation — it becomes a real-time index of globalization itself.
